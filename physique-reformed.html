Overview

What is Physique Reformed?

PhysiqueReformed is an All-In-One cloud storage platform built for coaches and clients. A new minimalistic designed approach for trainers to offer the best possible service they can for their clients and for clients to have access to our trainers’ whole arsenal of tools and information at their disposal at any location to help them in their fitness journey.

The Problem

A long-time client had come to me with an idea for a cloud storage app, but they had no vision of where to take the app or what market to focus on. They needed my help to find a niche for their app and help bring their app into the market.

A cloud storage app that would have the right combination of features that could compete in the existing market, such as organizing content, creating content, and uploading files. As well as including collaboration features like, sharing items or folders with others & connecting with other users in real-time. Also, I had to come up with the name and branding for the app & whether or not it should just be a web app for desktop browsers or be usable on mobile devices too.

The Solution

An easy to use mobile responsive/web app for desktop browsers that connects fitness personal trainers with their clients. It was an app that was meant to be usable for both trainers and clients tailored to Health and Fitness.

The application helps users share workout video tutorials, nutrition plans, workout plans, upload workouts, meals, exercises, recent activity, and collaborate for group training sessions or informative talks. It was designed to be minimalistic and provide the information most needed to fit each individual’s goals. Clients can seek out information on nutrition, workouts, guidance from trainers and support from other users. Trainers are there to grow their business and collaborate with other trainers that share the same passion to help people transform their lives through fitness.

The Process

Design Planning and Research

After defining the problem and identifying the solution, the next step involves design planning and research. In this phase, I was tasked with both UI & UX design. I conducted my User Research starting with user surveys, then doing a competitive analysis, creating user personas, user stories, and user flows. After that, I’ll build an inspirational mood board, sketch out ideas and build out wireframes, mockups, and branding work that solve for user requirements. Lastly, make final iterations on prototypes throughout the process from user testing and proceed with the final results to have as my deliverables.

Discovery & Research

User Surveys

I started by creating a Google Form survey, recruiting friends, family, and other participants through social media. The survey gathered information about what would be the main features that both trainers and clients would need in a fitness cloud storage serivce.

For my survey, I had 24 respondents.
Below are my key discoveries:

87.5% used cloud storage to save data.
12.5% did not use cloud storage to save data.
To my surprise, most used cloud storage for:
52.6% back-up keeping
21.1% each for stuff management and content creation
5.3% for Fitness.
81% of respondents use desktop primarily for cloud storage services.
66.7% of respondents use mobile devices for cloud storage services.
What features are mostly used?
95.2% uploading files
61.9% creating content
57.1% organizing content

There were untapped areas that could be useful as a niche to break into the market, such as fitness, storage for a trainer to keep up with clients and collaborate with them or for clients to access trainer information & updates anywhere.

With these things in mind, it gives an idea of what users want the most from a cloud storage app & would be useful when constructing user flows or an “MVP” for a starting project, such as for a Fitness Cloud Storage app.

Competitive Research

From the information gathered, my analysis was that for users depending on their needs it would determine which type of storage they would decide to use. I think if a new competitor were to enter the marketplace they could take advantage of the weakness and differences of each of its competitors. By targeting a specific niche market and taking the bits and pieces that thrive in its competitors while adding in some features of its own to help it stand out. With these things in mind, it gave an idea of what users want most from a cloud storage app & what would be useful when constructing an “MVP” for a starting project, such as a Fitness Cloud Storage app for Personal Trainers and their clients. To share content, upload workouts, meal tracking, and allow real-time collaboration with clients. Also, it can content to share with clients, such as documents, slides or videos. As well as offer a mobile app in the market, so that both trainers and clients can access their information on the go.

Personas

Edward Ruiz

Personal Trainer

Age: 23 / Location: San Diego, CA / Gender: Male

Motivation

Edward is a devoted online personal trainer to a vast amount of clients and for the past couple of months has found it hard to be able to keep up with each client individually. Edward wants to have a library of meals & workouts easily accessible to his clients to view & be able to host group collaboration meetings to teach multiple clients at once. Also, he would love for his clients to collaborate for additional support.

Frustration

Manual tracking new clients vs old clients progress can get cluttered. The market is saturated with over 30+ cloud-based apps making it hard to decide which is the best one. No time for group collaborations for clients to discuss progress.

“I want to be able to change the lives of many people through fitness.”

Christian Lopez

Warehouse Supervisor

Age: 20 / Location: San Diego, CA / Gender: Male

Motivation

Christian is full time supervisor for a warehouse and for the past couple of years has been interested in tracking aspects of his health. Christian wants to track his food and exercise and see how his eating and exercise habits affect his well being. Also, he would like to be able to save the information he receives from his trainer and be able to share his experience with others going through the same journey.

Frustration

Christian has no one to share his fitness journey with, no knowledge of what meals to eat or workouts to follow, and no knowledge of what health stats are healthy for him.

“I want to be able to change my health while maintaining a busy schedule”

Information Architecture

Breaking down the process

The next step was to put the knowledge from research in motion to define the app’s functions, user flows, and design the prototype.

User Stories

Trainer

Be able to create/sign in to an account.
Be able to access client information and updates.
Be able to do payment processing for clients.
Be able to do attendance tracking.
Be able to set up macronutrients for clients.
Be able to upload workouts for clients.

Client

Be able to create/sign in to an account.
Be able to log in workouts and meals.
Be able to view a library of workouts.

User Flows

Based on my research I conducted three users’ flow. All of which were included in my prototype. Overall flows were simple and straightforward.

1st - Sign-up for an account/Sign-In to account
2nd - Payment processing for subscription service
3rd - Access User (Trainer or Client) features

Digital Wireframes

The prototype was designed in two iterations. The first part of the design was presenting the user flows mapping out all the high-priority user stories included in the low-fi wireframes.

User Testing

I conducted a user test with my low-fi, clickable prototype with 3 users. I prepared the clickable low-fi prototype (made in Figma). The users had to go through 3 tasks, the whole task took around 5 minutes each to complete. The prototype was both held in person and online. For online users, they were given a “MAZE” link to the prototype where I viewed the results and screen recordings afterward. For in-person users the prototype was displayed on the monitor of the computer, users used a mouse to navigate and click. They were asked to think aloud.

Overall all of the participants were able to flow from one task to the other effortlessly with very little trouble on completing the task flows, so no iterations were needed to be made.

Early logo idea sketches!

User Testing Round 2

Changes were made to the design through preference testing, such as background colors, button styles, and layouts.

The participants had to complete the following tasks:

1. Sign up with a trainer account on the $ 50-month plan
2. Add a food item to the breakfast meal log for a client
3. Add an exercise starting from the client’s dashboard
4. From the trainer’s dashboard, view a client’s macros (In-person only task)

Overall, all of the users completed each of the tasks within about 1 minute & no further changes seemed evident that should be made to the prototype.

Through testing, I did discover that were inconsistencies reported by users, such as buttons and colors in the “sign-up” process. I later went back and made changes to those.

Conclusion

The most important lesson from this project was to always do research and user test.

During my research, I found that every person who is a client is looking for something different from a personal trainer. Some would need help with their nutrition, others would need help with their programming. Each type of client had a different need and desire for wanting to make a change in themselves through fitness for a healthy lifestyle. It was important for me as a designer to give as many opportunities that I could to help clients reach their goals, by offering macro setups, workouts builders, and a variety of different specialized personal trainers.

Also, it was just as important to think about the needs of personal trainers. To know what tools they need at their disposal to be able to help their client in every possible way. User testing is one other tool that helps us - UX Designers - create amazing products. I was able to see if any of the functions that I had built were going to be usable for my users.

Without having conducted research & user testing on both of these types of users I would not have been able to come up with a solution. The best type of research is with little assumptions as possible.
